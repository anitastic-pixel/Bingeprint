Smart Scoring

How this works

Two ways to read this: Start here gets you running in about five minutes; How it works explains how the engine thinks, one section at a time. Sections are independent — dip in as needed, skip the rest.

Smart Scoring is a private, local recommender built around your taste — not what's trending. Where Crunchyroll's rec row shows what's popular with the broader audience, Smart Score asks "is this for you?" and shows the reasoning. Nothing ever leaves your browser.

Getting started — the first 5 minutes

Three things will get you usable picks fast. Do them in any order; each one strengthens the engine on its own.

  1. Take the Quick Taste Check. Click the toolbar icon → "Quick taste check." Tap loved/skipped on shows you recognize. ~30 taps is enough to get the engine off zero.
  2. Connect AniList or MAL (optional). If you keep a list on either, your scores there are richer signal than the survey alone. Toolbar icon → "Profile completeness" → connect.
  3. Just browse Crunchyroll. Open any series page — you'll see your first Smart Score card. Open the side panel (sparkle ✦ in CR's top bar) for ranked picks.

The engine sharpens as data lands. First ~30 watched/rated shows, treat scores as directional. By ~80 the radar shape is honest and recommendations stabilize.

Where things live

The Smart Score card

On every Crunchyroll series page, Smart Scoring drops a card that tells you whether the show is for you. The big number is the Smart Score — a percentage match against your taste, where 100 = "this is your kind of show" and 0 = "really not."

The score gets a tier label so you don't have to memorize what counts as a high number. Each tier has its own color on the card:

TRUST ME WORTH A SHOT STRETCH PROBABLY NOT SKIP
  • TRUST ME — strong match (≥9.0). Watch tonight, no second-guessing.
  • WORTH A SHOT — solid pick (7.5+). Add to your queue.
  • STRETCH — outside your comfort but earned it (5.5+). A weekend project — you might love it once you commit.
  • PROBABLY NOT — skeptical (3.5+). Don't lead with this; only if a friend insists.
  • SKIP — confident no. Trust this once you have ~80 shows of signal.

Below the score sit a few chips — short, plain-language reasons, not a tag dump:

  • What's pulling for it — signals nudging the show toward your taste (Slice-of-life pacing, Found-family beats, Spellcasting).
  • What might bug you — patterns you've consistently dropped (Power-up formula, Heavy fanservice, Glacial pacing), so you know what you're walking into.

The wording is a friend-voice layer over the raw AniList tag ("Politics" → "Throne-room scheming"). Hover any chip to see the exact tag that fired. Studio and creator chips ("Madhouse track record") are coming in a later pass.

Heads up if you just installed: for the first ~30 watched/rated shows, treat scores as directional. The engine sharpens as it learns you — by ~80 it's honest enough to trust a SKIP.

Other badges you might see on the card

Beside the tier, the card surfaces small status chips that explain why a show has the standing it does. They don't all fire every time — most cards have one or two.

★ FAVORITE You marked this with the heart on Crunchyroll. Strongest "I love this" signal CR offers.
ON WATCHLIST You've saved this to your CR watchlist — explicit "I want to watch this" intent.
REWATCHED You've gone back through this. Costly explicit signal — the engine boosts taste match for shows like it.
★ YOUR FAVORITE You watched ≥90% AND came back. Top-tier verified favorite; the engine treats this as a peak personal pick.
CR PICKS · #7 Crunchyroll's own engine ranks this high for you. When their model and ours agree, that's a higher-confidence pick.
CR FAVORITE Crunchyroll's audience rates this notably higher than the broader AniList community.
CRITICS' PICK The AniList community rates this notably higher than CR's audience — often a niche-resonance / critics' favorite.
PEAK PEDIGREE Director + studio track record in the top quartile of your watched corpus. Craft pedigree is unusually high.
ENDS IN A HURRY Common rushed-adaptation shape — short ep count, no confirmed sequel. Pacing tends to crumple here.
EARLY GUESS Scored on your taste + this show's quality only — no community-recommendation signal yet. Tier is honest but a notch less confident.
LOW CONFIDENCE Thinner data backing the score: limited tags, sparse cross-source ratings. Could swing more than usual.
DEALBREAKER · isekai A tag you've blocked is central to this show. Surfaces only if you've set dealbreakers (Settings → Advanced).
How the Smart Score is computed (technical)

The score is a weighted blend of normalized sub-scores plus a creator-affinity additive lift. The exact mix depends on whether the show is in your recommendation pool — a series page often shows a title that isn't, so the card swaps AniList's community-rec signal for a Crunchyroll-collaborative one: 

Ranked Smart Picks (rec pool):
  finalScore = 0.55·tasteN + 0.30·recN + 0.15·qualN + lift

On-page card (show outside the rec pool):
  finalScore = 0.50·tasteN + 0.35·qualN + 0.15·crCF + lift
  (or 0.55·tasteN + 0.45·qualN + lift when there's no
   Crunchyroll-collaborative signal — the EARLY GUESS case)

  lift = max(0, creatorN − 0.5) · 0.10
  • tasteN — your Taste Vector's dot product with the show's tag profile. Roughly: how well the show's AniList tags align with the tag-mass you've accumulated from shows you liked.
  • recN — community-recommendation signal (ranked picks only). People who liked shows like this also liked X, so X is more likely a fit. Comes from AniList's recommendation graph.
  • crCF — Crunchyroll-collaborative signal (on-page card only). Where this show sits in Crunchyroll's own personalized list for you; shows CR doesn't rank resolve to a neutral 0.5 rather than a penalty.
  • qualN — AniList community average score / 100. The "is this generally well-regarded?" floor.
  • creatorN — affinity to the production team (studio + director + composer + character designer + series composition + original creator). Applied as an additive lift only when the team scores above the midpoint of your trusted teams; unknown teams default to neutral 0.5 with no penalty.

Then edge-anchored calibration: the raw blend gets remapped so the user's top 5% of scores reaches ≥0.90 and the bottom 5% reaches ≤0.35. Without this, raw distributions cluster mid-range and tier bands stay empty — every user always has TRUST ME and SKIP picks in their pocket.

Tier thresholds: ≥0.90 TRUST ME · ≥0.75 WORTH A SHOT · ≥0.55 STRETCH · ≥0.35 PROBABLY NOT · <0.35 SKIP.

Source of truth: per-show-score.js (taste cosine), rank-recommendations.js (rec-pool blend weights), all-shows-scoring.js (on-page card blend weights), score-blender.js (shared lift + clamp), score-normalizer.js (calibration), studio-creator-index.js (creator affinity).

The Smart Picks side panel

Click the sparkle button in Crunchyroll's top bar (or the toolbar icon → "Open side panel") to see your ranked Smart Picks. This is the engine's best guesses for what to watch next.

Eight lenses live here — each is a different cut of "what to watch next":

  • Peak — elevated, ambitious picks. Things to lean into.
  • Comfort — easier rewatch-y picks. Brain-off mode.
  • In the Air — currently-airing shows in your wheelhouse.
  • People You Trust — picks by studios and creators you've consistently loved.
  • Take a Chance — stretch picks outside your usual lanes.
  • You've Missed — high-quality classics you haven't watched yet.
  • Try Again — shows you dropped where current taste-fit suggests another shot.
  • Rewatched — shows you came back to: your personal-canon comfort picks, surfaced as their own lens.

Each card shows the score, the tier, and a one-line reason for the rec. Click a card to open that show on Crunchyroll.

Each card carries two distinct signals you can read at a glance:

  • Rank chip (#1, #2, #3, …) — your position in this lens today. Answers "why is this card here?" The list is curated for you, not just sorted by raw score.
  • Score number (top-right) — the engine's honest rating of the show in general, independent of how it stacks against your other picks. Same show, same score, everywhere it appears.

Because they answer different questions, they can diverge: a show can be your #1 today with a lower score than #2's, since the ranking weighs your taste shape, recent history, and any vibes. The rank is for you today; the score is for the show, always.

Vibe today — mood filter

Above the picks list there's a "Vibe today" row of mood tags (chill, hype, slow-burn, atmospheric, bittersweet, and so on). Tap a few to narrow the list to shows that fit the mood — without changing your underlying taste profile. Clear to see the unfiltered ranking again.

When vibes are selected, each card gets a small N/M vibes badge (how many of your picks it fires), and the list reorders so the strongest matches rise. Pick more vibes and a show needs more of them to qualify — roughly a third of your selection, rounded up.

How vibe matching works (technical)

Each vibe chip is a small bundle of AniList tag patterns — for example, melancholic maps roughly to Tragedy + Drama + Depression + Loss. Vibes don't touch your Taste Vector or the underlying scores; they're a display-time filter.

For every candidate, the filter scores how many selected vibes fire and how strongly each one fires (using AniList's per-tag rank, 0–100). Tag fires below rank 50 are dropped as noise — without this floor, a Tragedy tag ranked 30 on a battle-shounen would fire both bittersweet and melancholic, inflating its match count. Canonical genre fires (Action, Romance, etc.) always count. The matched set is sorted by weighted strength → match count → finalScore, so a show firing three vibes hard outranks one firing four weakly, and quality only breaks ties among similar-vibe cards.

A scaled minimum keeps big selections from spraying: ceil(selected / 3) vibes must fire before a show appears. Picking 2 vibes lets a 1-match through; picking 8 vibes demands at least 3. The threshold stays generous so narrow-but-strong matches (3 vibes firing hard on a contemplative show) survive instead of getting cut for not hitting half-of-eight.

Source of truth: vibe-tags.js (chip definitions and pattern map, vibeMatchDetail for per-rec scoring), sidepanel.js's render() for the filter and sort.

How Peak vs Comfort differ (technical)

Peak and Comfort aren't UI sorts — they're separate Taste Vectors built from different slices of your watch history.

The split happens at AniList community average score: 

peak    → only shows with averageScore ≥ 75 contribute
comfort → only shows with averageScore < 75 contribute

That means each lens learns from a different version of you. Your Peak vector reflects taste in shows the broader anime audience considers strong; your Comfort vector reflects what you reach for outside of those critically-validated picks. The same final-score formula runs against each, so a show can rank high on Peak and low on Comfort (or vice versa) depending on which slice of your library it best matches.

After scoring, the candidate list runs through a diversifier that prevents same-archetype clumping — without it, a strong shounen-action lean would fill the top 10 with near-identical recs.

Source of truth: taste-vector.js (peak/comfort split at line 104), diversify-recs.js, lens-pipeline.js.

Your Taste Shape

Switch to the "Shape" tab in the side panel to see your taste as a shape — a radar with eight axes. The longer the spike, the more you gravitate toward that thing.

The shape needs about 80 watched/rated shows before it's confident — until then we dim it and label it "Still learning your taste." Trust the strongest 2-3 spikes first; mid-rank axes are noisy at low signal.

How it's actually built (technical)

Every show you watch, rate, react to, or tap in the survey produces a Sentiment for that show — a single signed number with a confidence weight (e.g. { signal: +0.72, confidence: 0.85 }). Watch shape, reactions, ratings, watchlist intent, and external list scores all fold into that one number per show.

Each show carries a list of AniList tags. We multiply each tag's mass by the show's sentiment and accumulate across your whole library — that gives a single tag mass map: tag → how much you've invested in shows with that tag. Negative for tags associated with shows you dropped or rated low.

The eight radar axes are hand-curated tag bundles. For example, Spectacle Drive is: 

Spectacle = Action·1.0 + Battle·1.0 + Fight Scenes·1.5
          + Tournament·1.2 + Super Power·1.0
          + Martial Arts·1.0 + Mecha·0.8 + Magic·0.6
          + Sword Fighting·1.0 + Gun Action·1.0 + Chase·0.8

Each axis's raw value is the sum, across that axis's tags, of (your tag mass) × (axis tag weight), only counting positive mass. The eight raw values are then normalized against the largest one so the strongest axis anchors the outer ring at 1.0 and the others scale in proportion. That's why two users with very different absolute volumes can have legible radars on the same visual scale.

Confidence bands: the engine tracks a signalSeriesCount (completed + in-progress shows). Zero = cold (placeholder skeleton); below 8 = thin (caveat pill); ≥8 internally counts as calibrated. The Shape tab applies a stricter visual rule and dims the radar below ~80, since mid-rank axes get noisy at low signal even when the engine considers itself calibrated.

The eight axes:

  • Spectacle Drive — animation, action, visual energy
  • Narrative Depth — story structure, lore, mystery
  • Character Attachment — arcs, relationships, ensembles
  • Emotional Intensity — drama, tragedy, dark themes
  • Comfort Seeking — slice of life, healing, low stakes
  • Comedy / Chaos Appetite — gags, absurdism, chaotic pacing
  • Romance & Spice — chemistry, intimacy, ecchi tolerance
  • Curiosity Streak — auteur, prestige, niche, exploratory

Source of truth: radar-derive.js in the extension. Tag bundles + axis weights live in AXIS_DEFS; sentiment aggregation lives in the taste-vector pipeline.

The Quick Taste Check

The 3-minute onboarding survey. Tap shows you've loved or skipped — that's it. The engine learns enough from 30 taps to give you usable recs.

You can come back to it any time from the popup ("Quick taste check"). Your taps persist across sessions; you only "lose" them if you explicitly clear them.

How taps fold into the engine (technical)

There are two kinds of taps. Show taps (loved/skipped on a show tile) emit a per-series Sentiment contribution at moderate confidence — lower than a watched show, higher than nothing — so a real watch overrides a tap naturally as soon as it lands. Tag taps (loved/skipped on a tag chip) instead set a floor (loved) or ceiling (skipped) on that tag's mass in your Taste Vector, pinned to 0.6× of your strongest tag — "competitive but not dominant": enough to register as a real preference without overpowering what you've actually watched.

Because it's a floor rather than an additive bump, a tag tap short-circuits the show-aggregation step — handy for cold-starters who haven't watched anything on Crunchyroll yet. It also degrades gracefully: if your watched-show mass on that tag already clears the floor, the tap changes nothing (the survey screen calls this "noop — behavior stronger"). Floors are computed against your Peak vector, so a thin profile with no peak signal can't override yet.

Taps persist in chrome.storage.local.surveyShapes and surveyTagShapes. The engine recomputes 2 seconds after the most recent tap, so the success screen's top-3 picks are computed against your fresh taps.

Source of truth: survey-state.js (tap persistence), stated-preference.js (floor/ceiling override, FLOOR_FRACTION = 0.6), apply-flow.js (apply pipeline).

AniList & MAL imports

Don't recognize the names? AniList and MyAnimeList are anime-tracking sites where users score every show they've watched. If you don't keep a list on either, skip this whole section — Crunchyroll watch history + the Quick Taste Check is plenty.

If you keep a list on AniList (or MyAnimeList), connecting it gives the engine a much richer picture than the survey alone — actual scores on hundreds of shows, not just a few dozen taps.

From the popup → "Profile completeness," enter your AniList username (your list must be public) — the import runs automatically, no sign-in. Remove it any time from the same row. Private list? Use the MAL / AniList list (XML) import instead — export your AniList list to MAL-format XML with a converter and drop it in.

See Privacy for what we read. No login, no token — AniList import is just your public list.

How list data folds into the engine (technical)

Each list entry produces a Sentiment contribution. The score-to-signal mapping uses a fixed pivot at 7: 

signal = clamp((score − 7) / 3, −1, +1)

7 sits at the AL/MAL population median — most users score things they liked enough to track, so a 7 is "ambient average," not a positive endorsement. A score of 10 maps to +1.0; a 4 maps to −1.0; a 7 is neutral.

Status modulates confidence: 

completed       → confidence 0.60
dropped         → confidence 0.55
watching/paused → confidence 0.40
planning        → skipped (no signal)

If a status exists without a score, status alone contributes (completed: +0.20 / 0.40, dropped: −0.40 / 0.50). Every import confidence sits well below what an in-app Crunchyroll rating carries (~0.95+), so any rating you give on CR naturally overrides an imported score on the same show.

AniList import reads your public list by username via graphql.anilist.co — no login, no token stored. MAL (or AniList) data can also come from an XML export file you upload. Either way, no credentials are kept.

Source of truth: external-list-importer.js (import pipeline), anilist.js (public list fetch by username), CONTEXT.md "External score" mapping rules.

Privacy

Smart Scoring runs entirely in your browser. There are no servers, no telemetry, no analytics, and no accounts you sign up for.

What we read:

  • Your Crunchyroll watch history (titles, completion %, ratings) via the same authenticated session your browser already has.
  • Public AniList data about each show (tags, scores, recommendations).
  • A public anime database — manami-project's anime-offline-database (ODbL) — to match Crunchyroll titles to AniList entries and fill in tags (character archetypes, content warnings) that AniList covers sparsely.
  • If you enter your AniList username, your public AniList list — no sign-in, no token. Or a MAL/AniList list from an XML export file you upload.

Where the data lives:

  • All taste data lives in chrome.storage.local on your machine. Uninstalling the extension wipes it.
  • No login tokens or passwords are stored — list import is by public username or an uploaded file, never a credential.
  • Remove an imported list any time from the popup.

We don't have a server to send your data to. There's no "us" to leak it.

Browser support: Smart Scoring is built for Chromium browsers — Chrome, Edge, Brave, Arc, Opera. Firefox and Safari aren't supported in alpha (different extension API surfaces).

Backup & restore

The first sync is slow — Crunchyroll watch history takes time to fetch, AniList scoring imports take longer. Backup & restore lets you save all of that to a JSON file and load it again on another PC, after a reinstall, or just as insurance. It lives in the popup, below Engine status.

What you can include:

  • CR data — your watch history and per-show metadata.
  • AniList data — imported scores and link state (just your public-list data + username; no credentials).
  • Survey + reactions — Quick Taste Check taps, ratings, and reactions on shows.
  • Settings — dealbreakers, panel toggles, slider tunings.

All four checkboxes default on. Engine output (taste vector, scored shows, caches) auto-rides along with any checked bucket, so a restore lands instantly instead of recomputing. Click Save backup and crsmart-backup-YYYY-MM-DD.json downloads.

Open restore page opens a tab where you pick a file, preview what's in it (schema version, bucket counts, exported-when), and choose which buckets to restore. Anything you skip — or any bucket the file lacks — is left untouched, so a CR-only backup won't wipe AniList data already on the device.

The backup file is plain JSON — your taste data (scores, settings, archetype state). It holds no login credentials or tokens (AniList import is by public username), so there's nothing password-grade to guard.

How the format works (technical)

The file is a versioned envelope with a top-level schemaVersion integer and a buckets map. Each bucket carries a curated list of chrome.storage.local keys; synthetic / transient keys (_importState, _devAxisSandbox, _tourMetrics, _engineHealth) are never included. 

{
  "schemaVersion": 1,
  "exportedAt": "2026-05-08T...",
  "extensionVersion": "1.4.2",
  "buckets": {
    "cr":           { "watchShapes": {...}, "profileId": "..." },
    "anilist":      { "externalScores": {...}, "aniListMeta": {...} },
    "survey":       { "surveyShapes": {...}, "userRatings": {...} },
    "settings":     { "dealbreakers": [...], "surfaceSettings": {...} },
    "engineOutput": { "tasteVector": {...}, "allShowsScored": [...] }
  }
}

Smart-conditional engineOutput on import. The file's cached engine output is trusted only when the import covers every source bucket the device will end up with. So:

  • Fresh PC + "Save everything" backup → instant restore (~0s recompute).
  • Device has AniList linked + you import a CR-only file → file's engine output is discarded; pipeline recomputes (~10s) once writes land.
  • Settings-only restore → no source data changed; recompute is skipped entirely.

Atomic write. Affected keys get snapshotted before the import; the actual write is a single chrome.storage.local.set({...}). If the write throws (quota exhaustion, serialization error), the snapshot is restored and the engine state is exactly what it was before you clicked Restore. No half-applied imports.

Per-bucket replace. Imported buckets overwrite their keys; buckets not in the file are left untouched on the device. The AniList bucket carries your linked username + account record (no token), so restoring it re-links your public list on the new device — no sign-in needed.

Cross-version handling. Higher schemaVersion than the current build → reject with a clear "update the extension" error. Lower → migration chain (none yet, but the framework is in place). Unknown keys inside a known bucket are silently ignored and surfaced in the import summary.

Source of truth: backup-schema.js (envelope + smart-conditional rule), import.js (preview + atomic restore), popup.js (export panel).

Troubleshooting

Most "is it broken?" moments fall into one of these. If none of these match, the friction is probably worth reporting.

The Smart Score card isn't showing up on a show page.

Three common causes:

  • Page didn't fully load. CR's series pages render in stages — the card needs the page's metadata. Refresh once.
  • Engine still warming up on a fresh install. First load fetches AniList enrichment in the background. Wait a couple minutes, then refresh.
  • You're not signed into Crunchyroll. The extension reads your watch state via your CR session. Sign in, refresh.
The side panel won't open / the sparkle button is missing.

The sparkle ✦ button is injected into CR's top bar. If it's missing:

  • Refresh the CR page once.
  • Use the toolbar icon → "Open side panel" instead.
  • If neither works, open chrome://extensions, toggle Smart Scoring off and on, reload CR.
All my Smart Scores look about the same number.

The engine hasn't learned enough about you yet to spread the scores. In rough order of effectiveness:

  • Take the Quick Taste Check (~30 taps).
  • Connect AniList or MAL if you have a list.
  • Watch and rate a few shows on Crunchyroll.

Edge-anchored calibration kicks in once you've got real spread — it stretches your top picks into TRUST ME and your clear no's into SKIP, instead of leaving everything mid-band.

The toolbar icon is greyed out.

Greyed = the extension is loaded but has no active CR tab to attach to. Open any Crunchyroll page; the icon should light up within a second or two.

A show I love is scored low (or vice versa).

One miss isn't a bug — the engine averages across signal, so individual shows can be off-pattern. But two things help:

  • Open the show's card and tag a couple of Reactions. They tell the engine why it landed and tune future picks.
  • If a tag the show carries is genuinely a turnoff for you, set it as a Dealbreaker (Settings → Advanced).

FAQ

What happens to my data if I uninstall?

All of it goes. Everything lives in chrome.storage.local, which Chrome wipes on uninstall — there's no cloud copy because there's no cloud. Your taste vector, survey taps, ratings, and imported scores are all gone.

If you reinstall, your Crunchyroll watch history resyncs on its own (CR keeps it server-side, independent of the extension); the rest you'd rebuild. To skip that, take a backup first.

Can my partner and I share one Crunchyroll account?

Yes. Each of you installs the extension on your own machine (or your own Chrome profile if you share a computer). Storage is isolated per Chrome profile per machine, so neither of you sees the other's taste data even on the same CR account.

On Crunchyroll's side, watch history is keyed per profile, not per account — so different CR profiles give different histories and different taste shapes.

The picks keep changing — is something broken?

Usually not. Two things move the list:

  • You watched or rated a new show → the engine recomputes your taste vector and reranks.
  • On a fresh install, AniList enrichment keeps landing in the background → ranks update as show metadata fills in.

Give it a few hours after install for enrichment to finish; after that the list settles unless you add new signal.

It recommended a show I've never seen — how does it know about it?

The candidate pool comes from AniList's recommendation graph — roughly "people who liked the shows in your library also liked X." The extension pulls that graph and scores each candidate against your taste shape, so the recs include shows you've never opened on Crunchyroll.

The Smart Score is ours; the candidate pool isn't. We score what AniList says is adjacent to your library.

Anime & engine terms

Isekai
"Other world" — a protagonist transported to or reborn in a fantasy / game-like setting. Sword Art Online, Re:Zero.
Shounen / Seinen
Audience labels. Shounen = aimed at teen boys, often action with power-up arcs (Naruto, Demon Slayer). Seinen = aimed at adults, heavier and slower (Vinland Saga, Berserk).
Slice of life
Low-stakes everyday situations — vibes over plot. K-On!, Laid-Back Camp.
Iyashikei
"Healing" — a slice-of-life subgenre engineered to feel calming. Mushishi, Natsume's Book of Friends.
Ecchi
Sexually suggestive but non-explicit content; fanservice-heavy.
Mecha
Giant robots and piloted machines as a central element. Gundam, Evangelion.
Taste Vector
Your tag-mass map: how much positive (or negative) weight you've built up for each AniList tag, summed across everything you've watched, rated, and tapped.
Sentiment
One signed number per show (how much you liked it) plus a confidence weight. Watch %, ratings, reactions, and imports all fold into it.
